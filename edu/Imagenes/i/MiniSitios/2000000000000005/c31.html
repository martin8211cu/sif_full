Electivas

The Blue Valley School High School program consists of five basic core courses, other required courses, and electives. The students may choose when to take the required courses and which electives to take.

These are classified as work intensive or work supportive so that students can design their workload.

FIVE CREDIT ELECTIVES (five periods a week) are work intensive, involving tests and number grades.

Eleventh grade choices for core courses:

Math for Bachillerato. Designed to follow the specific contents for the MEP Bachillerato exams.

Matem 1. Basic college math. This course also prepares students for the Bachillerato exams. Tests may be taken at the UCR and a passing grade of 70% gives students college credit, but this is not mandatory.

Biología. Designed to follow the specific requirements for the MEP Bachillerato test, it is mostly a translation of terms learned in Biology class in ninth grade. It serves as the core course for science in eleventh grade.

Physics. An eleventh grade core course for science also prepares for "Física de Bachillerato", (mostly translation of terminology) in the Fall Semester. Students on the high school track are well advised to take Physics as the science core course in eleventh grade.

Química General. This core course for science meets the requirements for the first year college chemistry course at the Universidad de Costa Rica. It is in large part a translation of terms learned in Chemistry class in tenth grade to prepare for taking the "Bachillerato" test in Chemistry. Students

who choose to take their tests at the UCR can earn college credit .

• For graduation, students are required to take at least one semester in Philosophy ("I" describes a first semester course; "II" the second part, taught the following semester) and one in Psychology, suggested as core courses for Social Studies in tenth grade but ninth grade students who are willing to

manage a greater work load can also enroll.

Philosophy I.

The history of philosophical ideas of the classical period (in Sophie’s World) alternates with group discussion on philosophical issues. Students will work to articulate their own philosophy while studying the historical development of ideas and engaging in critical debate about contemporary ethical, metaphysical, epistemological, political, and religious issues.

Philosophy II. (not selected by students this semester)

Learning the history of philosophy is continued (from Philosophy I) in Sophie's World: addressing philosophers from the Middle Ages, the Renaissance, Scholastic and modern day. Discussions on philosophical themes are based on primary sources. All students who have taken Philosophy I can

take it as an elective.

Psychology I

An introductory course in Psychology includes biology and behavior; learning, thinking, and feeling; motivation, personality, and development. Students finish with a fine grasp of fundamental concepts psychological.

Child Psychology (not offered this semester)

Introduction of the theoretical perspectives of human development that goes from early childhood until early adulthood, imparted through teacher’s lectures, case studies, a research project and related exercises involving

the students’ own personality.

• Students on the High School Diploma track are required to take U.S. History during one year.

United States History I

The first semester covers through the nineteenth century.

United States History II

The second semester is also called Twentieth Century History since the increasing hegemony of the United States touches upon most of the world’s affairs.

• For graduation, students are required to take at least one complementary five credit course in any

of their three years. Besides those described above, the following are five credit courses.

Economics I Essentials of Economics

Based on the book Essentials of Economics, the students will learn how markets work and how to make choices, considering scarce resources, on what to produce, how to produce it, and for whom.

Latin. (not selected by students this semester)

The students will learn about the Latin language’s presence in both the Spanish and the English languages. They will study etymology (Latin affixes and base words), declensions to be able to look up Latin words in a dictionary, and Latin phrases that have transferred without change into both languages.

Literature through Drama. (not selected by students this semester)

This course is may be taken as a substitute of the English core course by students who need to take another course during their regular Literature course. They will be accepted after an analysis of their previous performance. The textbook is Drama, An Introduction to Literature and Performing Arts. Students will present one or more plays under the direction of Mr. Hawkins.

World History.

A course that all students may take as the social studies core course (e.g. tenth grade core course, or instead

of MEP courses if they prepare on their own for MEP tests) it is based on discussions of world affairs.

THREE CREDIT ELECTIVES are also work intensive, involving tests and number grades.

• Civics is a three lesson a week course (including the Wednesday conference). It complements the eleventh grade social studies course so it provides no extra credit. The Civics course is a graduation requirement for the Bachillerato Diploma. All students in eleventh grade take Civics for the "Bachillerato" test

Cívica

Pretende la toma de conciencia de los valores, costumbres, y tradiciones que sustentan la nacionalidad a través del conocimiento de nuestras instituciones. Se enfatizan los deberes y derechos del ciudadano.

Administration (3) (not selected by students this semester)

The course will awaken an interest for management. The students will learn the principles of administration (planning, directing, organizing and control), theories in administration, basics of accounting, marketing, finances, and production.

United Nation Model (3 through two lessons a week and attendance to meetings)

Students learn the basics of parliamentary procedure and study current events in order to participate in from one to three Model United Nations that are held throughout the year 2002. For those who qualify, this class culminates in a weeklong trip to Boston in December. Parents cover expenses. Being able to travel is not a requirement for the course and taking the class does not warrant participation in international affairs. This is

based on experience, ability, and space availability.

TWO CREDIT ELECTIVES OR ONE CREDIT ELECTIVES are defined by (2) or (1) according to the number of

required lessons a week.

Parques Nacionales

Coonocimiento de nuestros parques a través de visitas, películas y literatura para comprender las leyes que los rigen y las ventajas que aportan al país.

Mitología (2) (not selected by students this semester)

Se estudiarán los mitos griegos y romanos y sus implicaciones en la literatura.

Hidroponía (2) (not selected by students this semester)

Los estudiantes realizan el cultivo de plantas en soluciones acuosas con soporte arenoso para aprender la teoría de la nutrición vegetal.

Astronomía (2) (not selected by students this semester)

Conocimientos científicos, filósoficos y prácticos derivados de la observación (audivisual la mayor parte del tiempo) de la esfera celeste.

Desarrollo Sostenible (2) (not selected by students this semester)

El curso pretende despertar en los estudiantes el interés por el tema del desarrollo sostenible como solución al deterioro ambiental que se experimenta en el planeta.

Producción más Limpia (2) (not selected by students this semester)

Se analizan diferentes empresas de producción del país y las normas para hacer menos contaminante la producción.

Las leyes (1)

Origen de las leyes (códigos, derecho romano, etc.), leyes constitucionales y leyes por decreto,

trámites para aprobar o modificar las leyes en Costa Rica.

Nutrición (1)

Para llegar a interpretar las etiquetas y comprender el valor nutricional de los alimentos y las recetas, los alumnos estudian la composición de los alimentos (qué tienen y para qué sirven) y el metabolismo (calorías, dieta balanceada y ejercicio). Analizaran diferentes formas de desórdenes alimenticios y sus consecuencias.

Recycling (1)

The students carry out a recycling project in the school, learning about its advantages and communicating them

to the rest of the population.

• Work Supportive

Students need eighteen extra credits, which they may obtain from the courses above when they are not taken as a core course. Also from the courses following, graded pass or fail, with no formal homework.

Thesis (2)

Students learn the process of writing a thesis (e.g. an extension of a term paper) leading to a thesis presentation, if they decide to graduate with honors by presenting an extended essay. When they do, they get 5 credits.

English Usage (1)

Recommended for students who have had little training in writing in English but some students will be assigned by previous teachers who know they should improve their composition level. Students come to rewrite the papers that their core teachers have graded and the teacher gives individual attention to three students per period, pointing out their recurrent mistakes and giving instructions to correct them.

Research

The students work on term papers with the guidance of a teacher.

Redacción. Composición. Ortografía. Técnicas de Estudio.

Aparecen como "Curso de Ms. Brenes" pues se decide a cuál debilidad se orienta una vez conformado el grupo. Se recomienda el curso a estudiantes con debilidad en alguna area, pero es obligatorio para algunos estudiantes

cuyo profesor considera indispensable que lo lleven

• Technological

Four credits in technological matters are required for graduation: two semesters of two credit courses or four semesters of one-credit courses.

Programación

Los estudiantes aprenderán a programar con el lenguaje "visual basics".

Digital Publications

Students design animation projects and use software applications to produce magazines, newspapers, yearbooks, and brochures using Quark, Ray Dream Studio, Quicktake, Adobe Illustrator, and Photoshop. On the Thursday and Friday class at noon, Ms. Ruenes will be working with students specifically on the yearbook.

Typing

Students in high school who cannot type using all their fingers must take this course. It is self-governed with weekly teacher control over progress and may be taken for one, two or three periods a week depending

on computer availability.

• Arts

Four credits in art are required for graduation: two semesters of two credit courses or four one credit semesters.

Plastic Arts

Drawing and painting, Art may be taken once a week, or twice if space is available.

Advanced Art

Art theory and appreciation, complemented by visits to museums and galleries, encourage the students’ creativity and ability to produce works that include a term project.

Music: Note-reading and Creative Applications

A step-by-step progressive reading program in which students practice not-reading in keyboard and rhythm readings for recorder, UCR drum rhythms. They also perform individual creative compositions written by them in a pentagram.

Music Appreciation (1-2). (not selected by students this semester)

Students will listen to music, classical and semi-classical and discussion will be help based on the teacher’s

instruction.

Historia del Arte

Visitas virtuales a museos y comentarios posteriores para estudiar los principales rasgos del devenir artístico mundial.

Creative Writing (not offered this semester)

The students learn to write based on a creative, imaginative response as well as to develop, edit, and analyze what they have written. The course focuses on imagination, freedom of expression, and spontaneity.

Narrativa (1) (not offered this semester)

Un viaje a través de la función creativa de la literatura en donde los alumnos analizan textos, identifican recursos y los aplican a sus propias creaciones.

Percepción Creativa (1) (not offered this semester)

Se incrementa la capacidad perceptiva y analítica mediante la exploración de un tema, escogido por el estudiante, con ayuda de formas abstractas producto de estímulos a los sentidos y emociones.

Drama (1)

Students will participate to put on a show as actors or technicians. As they are doing it they will learn about drama as a literary genre.

Danza (1) (not selected by students this semester)

Folkloric dances Costa Rica and other Latin American countries.

• Physical Fitness

Condición física

Ejercicios de gimnasia y recomendaciones para mejorar la condición física de las mujeres.

Voleibol

Especialmente útil a quienes deseen utililizar un período de educación física para otra electiva.

• Formative and Community Service credits

Students need to take one formative per semester and four community service periods for graduation.

Ethics is a required course per semester; therefore a period has been assigned for each level. Nevertheless, when students find it very important to use that period for a several credit course, they may attend Ethics session with another level during a more convenient period.

Community Service credit may be obtained by attending periods specified in the electives schedule, participating in the Saturday Community Project, or working as a teacher's aide.

Community Service projects:

Hogar Magdala

Students visit a home for children (some over thirty years old) with cerebral palsy, entertain them, and raise funds to provide for their needs.

Hogar de Ancianos

Students visit a home senior citizens and offer them company and/or entertainment.

Recycling

This course may also provide Community Service credits.

ADA

Students work with second and third grade students from the Guachipelín public school. Our students will read and hear them read stories to develop fluency, increase their vocabulary and encourage enjoyment of reading. The Amigos del Aprendizaje Project is a national program already involving ten schools in the Central Valley, including ours.

Saturday Project

Every other Saturday, students work for four hours with less privileged communities for two credits.

Teacher Aides

Students who have a non-assigned period during elective blocks may request a teacher's aide position in elementary or middle school. They must report promptly and be active throughout the lesson to earn credit.
